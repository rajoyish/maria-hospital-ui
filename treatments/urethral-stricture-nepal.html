Urethral Stricture Treatment

Do you feel like your urine flow is weak or slower than usual? You might be experiencing a condition called Urethral Stricture.

Think of the urethra (the tube that carries urine out of the body) like a garden hose. If there is a kink or narrowing in the hose, the water cannot flow freely. A urethral stricture is essentially scar tissue that narrows this tube, restricting the flow of urine. While this can happen to anyone, it is most common in men.

Common Signs and Symptoms

Symptoms can appear suddenly or develop slowly over time. If you notice any of the following, it might be time for a doctor consultation:

Weak Urine Flow
The stream is slow, or you have to strain to empty your bladder.
Incomplete Emptying
Feeling like there is still urine left even after you finish.
Spraying
The urine stream sprays or splits into two.
Pain
Discomfort in the lower abdomen or pain while urinating.
Frequent Infections
Recurring Urinary Tract Infections (UTIs).
Blood
Noticing blood in your urine or semen.

Important: If you suddenly cannot pass urine at all, this is a medical emergency. Seek immediate help to prevent kidney damage.

What Causes the Scar Tissue?

Strictures are usually caused by inflammation or injury. Common causes include:

  • Injury: Falling on the pelvic area or "straddle injuries."
  • Infection: Sexually transmitted infections (STIs) like gonorrhea or chlamydia.
  • Medical Procedures: Previous surgeries or the use of urinary catheters.
  • Unknown Causes: Sometimes, strictures form without a clear reason.

Diagnosis: How We Find the Problem

If you are worried about your symptoms, a health checkup is the first step. Your doctor may recommend a few simple tests for an accurate diagnosis:

Urine Flow Test
A simple machine measures the speed of your urine stream.
Ultrasound
We check how much urine remains in the bladder after you use the restroom.
X-Ray (Retrograde Urethrogram)
A special dye helps us see the exact location and size of the narrowing. [Image of Retrograde Urethrogram x-ray showing urethral stricture]
Cystoscopy
A thin camera allows the doctor to look directly inside the urethra.

Effective Treatment Options

Medication alone usually cannot fix a stricture; the narrowing often needs to be physically opened. Fortunately, effective treatment is available right here in Kathmandu in Nepal.

1. Dilation (Widening)

This is a simple procedure where the doctor gently stretches the narrowed area using progressively larger tubes or a balloon. It is often done under local anesthesia.

2. Urethrotomy (Cutting)

Using a specialized scope, the doctor makes a small cut in the scar tissue to widen the gap. A catheter is temporarily placed to help the area heal.

3. Urethral Reconstruction (Urethroplasty)

For severe or recurring strictures, reconstruction surgery is often the best long-term solution. Surgeons remove the scar tissue and reconnect the healthy parts of the urethra. In some cases, a small graft from the cheek lining is used to rebuild the tube. [Image of urethroplasty surgery illustration using buccal mucosa graft]

4. Drug-Coated Balloon

This is a newer method where a balloon stretches the urethra while delivering medication directly to the tissue to prevent the scar from growing back.

Prevention Tips

  • Practice Safe Sex: Use protection to avoid STIs that cause inflammation.
  • Protect Yourself: Be careful during sports or activities to avoid pelvic injury.
  • Gentle Care: If you use catheters, handle them gently with plenty of lubrication.
