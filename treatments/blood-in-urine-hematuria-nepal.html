Blood in Urine (Hematuria) Treatment

Seeing blood in your urine can be a very scary experience. It is natural to feel worried, but please remember: you are not alone. This is a common condition that doctors see frequently.

While it can sometimes signal a serious issue, in many cases, the cause is minor and easily treatable. At Maria Hospital, located in Kathmandu, Nepal, we are dedicated to helping you understand your symptoms and providing the right care.

What Is Hematuria?

"Hematuria" is the medical term for blood in the urine. It means that the kidneys or another part of your urinary tract (bladder, tubes, etc.) are allowing blood cells to leak into your urine.

There are two main types:

  • Visible (Gross) Hematuria: You can see this with your naked eye. Your urine may look pink, bright red, or brownish (like tea or cola).
  • Invisible (Microscopic) Hematuria: The urine looks normal to you, but a small amount of blood is found when a doctor tests your urine under a microscope during a health checkup.

Signs and Symptoms to Watch For

The most obvious sign is a change in the color of your urine. However, depending on the cause, you might also feel:

  • Pain or burning when you pass urine.
  • Frequent urination or a sudden, strong urge to go.
  • Back or side pain (often linked to kidney issues).
  • Fever or chills, which usually suggests an infection.
  • Blood clots in the urine, which can be painful.

Common Causes of Blood in Urine

Blood can come from anywhere in your urinary system. Common causes seen by doctors in Nepal include:

  • Urinary Tract Infections (UTIs): Bacteria enter the bladder or kidneys, causing infection, pain, and bleeding.
  • Kidney or Bladder Stones: Minerals in the urine form hard stones. When these stones move or get stuck, they cause severe pain and bleeding.
  • Enlarged Prostate: In older men, the prostate gland can grow and press on the urinary tube, causing bleeding.
  • Trauma: An injury from an accident or sports contact.
  • Medications: Some blood thinners or antibiotics can increase bleeding risks.
  • Cancer: Though less common, visible blood can be a sign of kidney or bladder cancer. Note: This is often painless, so do not ignore blood just because it doesn't hurt.

Did you know?

Not all red urine is blood. Foods like beets or blackberries, and certain medicines, can turn urine pink. However, it is always safer to get a doctor consultation to be sure.

Diagnosis: How We Find the Cause

At Maria Hospital, we use advanced technology to get an accurate diagnosis. Your checkup may include:

  • Urinalysis: A simple test to check your urine for infection, red blood cells, and protein.
  • Medical History: Your doctor will ask about your health history and risk factors (like smoking).
  • Imaging (Ultrasound or CT Scan): These safe scans help us "see" inside your body to spot stones or tumors.
  • Cystoscopy: If needed, a doctor uses a tiny camera tube to look directly inside your bladder.

Treatment Options at Maria Hospital

Your treatment plan depends entirely on what is causing the bleeding:

  • For Infections: We prescribe antibiotics to clear the bacteria.
  • For Kidney Stones: Small stones may pass with water; larger ones might need sound-wave therapy or minor procedures to remove them.
  • For Prostate Issues: Medication can help shrink the prostate.
  • For Cancer: Our specialist team will create a comprehensive plan involving surgery or therapy.

When Should You See a Doctor?

Do not wait. If you see blood in your urine, book a doctor consultation immediately. Even if it happens only once, or if you have no pain, it needs to be checked.

Seek help immediately if:

  • You cannot urinate at all.
  • You see blood clots.
  • You have severe pain or a high fever.
  • You have had a physical injury to your back or stomach.
