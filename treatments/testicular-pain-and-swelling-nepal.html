Testicular Pain and Swelling Treatment

Testicular pain or swelling can be very worrying for any man. Because this area is sensitive, even a small injury can hurt a lot. We understand that many men feel shy or embarrassed to talk about these problems, but ignoring pain in the scrotum is dangerous.

At Maria Hospital, we want you to know that most causes are treatable. However, some conditions need immediate medical attention to prevent permanent damage. Your health is our priority.

When Is It an Emergency?

Do not wait if you have severe symptoms. You should seek immediate doctor consultation or go to the Emergency Room if you experience:

  • Sudden, severe pain in one or both testicles.
  • Pain accompanied by nausea or vomiting.
  • Swelling, redness, or if the testicle feels like it is sitting higher than usual.
  • Pain caused by a serious injury or accident.

Important: If you have testicular pain that lasts for more than an hour—even if it isn't severe—you should book a health checkup immediately.

Common Causes of Pain and Swelling

There are several reasons why you might feel pain or see swelling. Here is a simple breakdown:

1. Testicular Torsion (Requires Immediate Action)

This happens when the cord that brings blood to the testicle twists. It cuts off the blood supply.

  • Symptoms: Sudden severe pain, swelling on one side, and nausea.
  • Action: This is an emergency. Surgery is usually needed within 6 hours to save the testicle.

2. Infection and Inflammation

Infections are a common reason for doctor consultation at our hospital.

Epididymitis
Inflammation of the tube at the back of the testicle. It is often caused by bacteria or urinary tract infections. You may feel a warm, swollen scrotum.
Orchitis
Swelling of the testicle itself, often caused by a virus (like mumps) or bacteria. It can cause fever.

3. Lumps, Cysts, and Hernias

Varicocele
Swollen veins inside the scrotum. It often feels like a "bag of worms" and causes a dull ache.
Hydrocele
Fluid buildup around the testicle. It causes painless swelling and is common in both babies and adults.
Inguinal Hernia
When tissue pushes through a weak spot in your muscles into the groin. You might see a bulge when you stand up.

4. Injuries and Trauma

A direct hit to the groin during sports or an accident can cause pain and bruising. While rest helps, severe injuries need a proper diagnosis to ensure there is no internal bleeding.

How We Diagnose the Problem

At Maria Hospital, located in Kathmandu in Nepal, we make the process comfortable and quick. To find the cause, a doctor will:

  • Ask about your history.
  • Perform a gentle physical exam.
  • Use an Ultrasound scan (a painless imaging test) to check blood flow and look for lumps.
  • Perform a urine test to check for infections.

Treatment Options

Your treatment depends on what is causing the pain:

  • Antibiotics: Used if you have a bacterial infection.
  • Surgery: Necessary for emergencies like torsion or severe hernias.
  • Home Care (The RICE Method): For minor injuries, doctors often suggest:
    • Rest: Avoid heavy lifting or sports.
    • Ice: Apply an ice pack for 15–20 minutes.
    • Compression: Wear supportive underwear.
    • Elevation: Lie down when possible.

Prevention and Self-Care

Taking care of your health is easy with these steps:

  • Wear Protection: Use a protective cup when playing sports.
  • Self-Exams: Check your testicles once a month for any new lumps.
  • Safe Habits: Use protection during sex to prevent infections that cause pain.

Don't Wait—Get Help Today

If you notice a lump, swelling, or pain, do not try to "tough it out." Early diagnosis often leads to easier and faster recovery.
