What Exactly is Acupuncture?

At its core, acupuncture is an ancient medical practice where a trained specialist carefully inserts ultra-thin, sterile needles into specific points on your body. Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) revolves around the idea that a vital energy called "Qi" (pronounced "chee") flows through invisible pathways in your body known as meridians.

According to this philosophy, good health comes from a delicate balance between your body's opposing forces of Yin and Yang. When this energy flow is blocked or unbalanced, it can lead to pain or illness. By targeting specific points along your meridians, acupuncture aims to unblock these channels and get your energy flowing smoothly again so your body can heal itself.

How Does It Actually Work?

While Eastern traditions focus on energy flow, Western medicine explains acupuncture through its impact on the nervous system. Studies suggest that inserting these needles stimulates your central nervous system, prompting your brain and spinal cord to release endorphins, which act as your body's natural painkillers. On top of that, acupuncture can boost blood circulation, regulate your body temperature, and even strengthen your immune system.

The Core Sequence of TCM Healing

A crucial principle in our Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) health preservation and treatment is following the correct sequence of conditioning: “Clear → Unblock → Regulate → Tonify → Consolidate” (rather than blindly nourishing first). This step-by-step approach addresses both root causes and symptoms, avoiding improper supplementation that may lead to “deficiency that cannot absorb tonification” or “trapping pathogens inside the body.”

  • 1. Clear (Qing): Remove toxins and pathological factors from the body, such as damp-heat, phlegm accumulation, and blood stasis. This is the first step—like a “deep cleansing” of the body—laying the foundation for subsequent treatment.
  • 2. Unblock (Tong): Promote the smooth flow of meridians, qi, blood, and the Sanjiao (triple burner). Only when pathways are unobstructed can nutrients be delivered and waste be eliminated. As the TCM saying goes: “Where there is free flow, there is no pain or stagnation.”
  • 3. Regulate (Tiao): Restore balance to organ functions and harmonize yin and yang. After clearing and unblocking, underlying imbalances become clearer, allowing targeted adjustments of organs such as the liver, spleen, and kidneys.
  • 4. Tonify (Bu): Replenish qi, blood, and body fluids based on individual constitution. At this stage, supplementation can be properly absorbed and utilized, avoiding ineffective or counterproductive tonification.
  • 5. Consolidate (Gu): Strengthen the foundation, reinforce vital energy, and maintain results. This helps prevent recurrence and ensures the long-term stability of your health.

What to Expect During Your Session

During your very first appointment, your acupuncturist will take the time to chat with you about your health history, current symptoms, lifestyle, and diet. As part of a traditional diagnosis, they might also check the color and shape of your tongue and feel the pulse in both of your wrists.

When it is time for the treatment, you will lie comfortably on a padded table. The practitioner will then insert fine, sterile, single-use stainless steel needles into specific points. Because the needles are incredibly thin, you will likely feel very little discomfort. You might notice a mild ache, a slight tingling, or a feeling of heaviness. In Chinese medicine, this sensation is known as "De-qi," and it simply means the treatment is successfully activating your energy.

Typically, you will rest with the needles in place for 10 to 30 minutes. Most people find this incredibly relaxing, often leaving the session feeling either deeply calm or wonderfully energized.

Small Needle Knife Therapy vs. Acupuncture

In addition to traditional methods, we also utilize small needle knife therapy. Here is how it compares to traditional acupuncture:

Feature Small Needle Knife Acupuncture
Tools Needle tip with a tiny blade Ordinary needle
Principle Release adhesions, relieve pressure Stimulate acupoints, regulate meridians
Trauma Minimally invasive (slightly larger than acupuncture) Almost non-invasive
Applications Soft tissue adhesions, chronic pain Conditioning and pain management
Operation Requires higher doctor skill Relatively common

In short, acupuncture provides "stimulation," while small needle knife therapy provides "release + treatment". Small needle knife therapy is a minimally invasive adhesion release that is highly suitable for chronic musculoskeletal pain.

Does Small Needle Knife Therapy Hurt?

Most people feel a little pain, but it is generally tolerable. This is because local anesthesia is sometimes used, and the operation time is very short, usually taking just a few minutes. Many people describe the feeling as a deep needling sensation with slight soreness, swelling, and tightness. After treatment, there may be soreness and distension for 1 to 3 days.

Best Treatment Applications for Small Needle Knife

The most common problems where mini-needle knife therapy is most effective are neck and shoulder pain, lumbar muscle strain, frozen shoulder, tendon sheath inflammation, tennis elbow, and heel pain. If the problem is a severe herniated disc or a bone structure issue, the effect may not be as good.

Safety and Precautions

Acupuncture is incredibly safe when performed by a licensed professional using sterile needles. You might experience some minor side effects like slight soreness, a tiny bit of bleeding, or light bruising where the needles were placed.

However, it is important to give your practitioner a heads-up before treatment if any of the following apply to you, as certain treatments (including small needle knife therapy) may not be recommended:

  • You have a bleeding disorder, poor blood clotting function, or are taking blood-thinning medication.
  • You have a pacemaker, as the electrical pulses in electroacupuncture can interfere with it.
  • You are pregnant, as certain acupuncture points can trigger uterine contractions, and small needle knife therapy is generally not recommended.
  • You have severe diabetes.
  • You have a local skin infection.
  • You have severe osteoporosis.
  • You are very sensitive to or fear pain.
