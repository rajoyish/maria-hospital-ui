Blood in Stool (Rectal Bleeding)

Seeing blood in your stool or on the toilet paper is definitely a scary experience, but please try not to panic. It is actually a very common issue. Most of the time, it comes from simple and easily treatable things like piles (hemorrhoids). Even so, it is always a smart idea to play it safe and get a professional health checkup.

Here at Maria Hospital in Kathmandu, Nepal, our caring doctors are ready to listen to your concerns, figure out exactly what is going on, and get you the right treatment so you can feel healthy and comfortable again.

What the Color of the Blood Can Tell Us

The color of the blood is a great clue for our medical team. It helps point us in the right direction for your diagnosis:

  • Bright red blood: This usually means the bleeding is happening very low in your digestive system, like in the rectum or anus.
  • Dark red or maroon blood: This often points to something happening a little higher up in the large intestine.
  • Black, tar-like stool: This can be a sign of bleeding from your upper digestive system, like your stomach.

A quick tip: Sometimes, things you eat (like beetroot or blueberries) or taking iron supplements can turn your stool red or black. This is completely safe and normal. If you are ever unsure, booking a doctor consultation is the best way to get peace of mind!

Common Causes We See Every Day

There are quite a few reasons why you might be experiencing this. Here are some of the most common issues we treat at Maria Hospital:

  • Piles (Hemorrhoids): These are just swollen blood vessels near the anus. They are the top cause of bleeding and usually show up as painless, bright red spots when you strain on the toilet.
  • Anal Fissures: These are tiny tears in the delicate skin of the anus, usually from passing hard stools. They tend to cause a sharp pain when you go to the bathroom.
  • Diverticular Disease: As we get older, little pouches can form in the lining of our intestines. Sometimes these can cause sudden bleeding without any pain.
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD): This includes conditions that cause swelling in the digestive tract, often bringing along stomach cramps and bloody diarrhea.
  • Polyps: These are small growths inside the colon. Catching them early with a routine health checkup makes them incredibly easy to treat and prevents more serious issues later on.

When You Should Seek Immediate Care

Most minor bleeding is not an emergency. But you should definitely head to the hospital right away if you notice any of these warning signs:

  • Continuous or heavy bleeding, or large blood clots in the toilet.
  • Stools that are black, sticky, or smell unusually bad.
  • Feeling dizzy, confused, or like you might pass out.
  • A fast heartbeat, chest pain, or difficulty breathing.
  • Severe stomach pain or cramping.

How We Figure Out What is Going On

Your comfort and privacy mean everything to us. Finding the root cause starts with a simple, pressure-free doctor consultation. Here is what we usually do:

  • We sit down, listen to your medical history, and do a gentle physical examination.
  • We might run some basic blood tests or check a stool sample.
  • If necessary, we might suggest a safe camera test (like a colonoscopy) to look inside your digestive tract so we can give you a completely accurate diagnosis.

Treatment and Prevention

How we treat the bleeding depends entirely on what is causing it, but getting relief is often much simpler than people expect.

  • Home Care: For minor piles or small tears, simply soaking in a warm bath and using over-the-counter soothing creams can do wonders.
  • Dietary Changes: The best way to prevent digestive issues in Nepal is to stick to a traditional diet packed with fiber. Eating plenty of green vegetables, lentils, and whole grains while drinking lots of water keeps your digestion smooth and stops you from straining.
  • Medical Procedures: For more stubborn issues, our doctors can perform quick, simple procedures to treat piles or safely remove polyps. Other conditions might just need a specific prescription medication.
