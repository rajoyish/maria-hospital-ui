Rectal Prolapse Treatment

Experiencing discomfort or noticing a bulge slipping from the anal area can be alarming and embarrassing. Please know that you are not alone, and this is a highly treatable condition known as rectal prolapse.

What is Rectal Prolapse?

The rectum is the lowest part of your large intestine. When the muscles and ligaments holding it in place weaken, the rectum can slip down, turn inside out, or stick out through the anal opening.

There are three main types of rectal prolapse:

  • Partial (Mucosal) Prolapse: Only the inner lining pushes out, usually when you use the bathroom.
  • Complete Prolapse: The entire wall of the rectum slides out of the body.
  • Internal Prolapse: The rectum folds inside itself but does not stick out.

Is it Just a Hemorrhoid?

It is very common to mistake rectal prolapse for hemorrhoids, as both cause painful lumps and bleeding. However, hemorrhoids are swollen blood vessels near the surface, while a prolapse is a larger segment of the bowel falling down.

While hemorrhoids often heal on their own, rectal prolapse requires a proper health checkup and will eventually need surgery to fix.

Common Symptoms to Watch For

In the beginning, the bulge might only appear when you use the bathroom and then go back inside on its own. Over time, it may stay outside or happen simply from standing or walking.

Common symptoms include:

  • A reddish, fleshy bulge coming from the anus.
  • Trouble controlling bowel movements (leakage of stool or mucus).
  • Feeling like you haven't fully emptied your bowels.
  • The physical sensation of "sitting on a ball."
  • Bleeding, pain, or itching in the anal area.

What Causes It?

Rectal prolapse happens when the pelvic floor muscles weaken. It is most common in adults over 50, and women are six times more likely to develop it than men.

Factors that increase your risk include:

  • Long-term constipation and straining on the toilet.
  • Chronic diarrhea.
  • Weakened muscles from aging or the physical stress of childbirth.
  • Spinal injuries or nerve damage in the pelvic area.

Accurate Diagnosis at Maria Hospital

Getting the right diagnosis is the first step toward relief. During your visit, your doctor will gently review your medical history and perform a basic physical exam.

To plan the most effective treatment, we may use a few painless tests to understand exactly what is happening:

  • Defecography: A specialized X-ray or MRI video to see how your pelvic muscles work.
  • Anorectal Manometry: A simple test to measure the strength of your muscles.
  • Colonoscopy: A quick camera check of your colon to rule out other issues.

Effective Treatment Options

For children, treating the root cause (like fixing constipation with diet and medicine) is often enough. For adults, surgery is usually the best and safest way to fix the problem permanently.

Based on your age and overall health, your surgeon will recommend the best approach:

  1. Abdominal Surgery (Rectopexy): Best for generally healthy patients. Using small, minimally invasive incisions, the surgeon pulls the rectum back into place and secures it.
  2. Perineal Surgery (Through the Anus): A faster-recovering option for older adults or those who are frail. The prolapsed part is carefully removed or stitched from the outside to reinforce the muscle wall.

Preventing Rectal Prolapse

You can protect your pelvic health and prevent the condition from returning by building these simple daily habits:

  • Eat a high-fiber diet rich in fresh local fruits, vegetables, and whole grains.
  • Drink 6 to 8 glasses of water every day.
  • Avoid straining on the toilet or doing heavy lifting.
  • Practice pelvic floor exercises (Kegel exercises) to keep your muscles strong.
