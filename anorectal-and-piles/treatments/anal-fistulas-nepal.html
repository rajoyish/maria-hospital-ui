Here is a simple guide to understanding anal fistulas and how we can help.

What is an Anal Fistula?

An anal fistula is a small, unnatural tunnel that forms between the inside of your anal canal (the end of your bowel) and the skin on the outside, near your anus.

What Causes It?

Inside the anus, there are tiny glands that make mucus. In about 80% of cases, an anal fistula happens when one of these glands gets blocked and infected.

  • Anal Abscess: This infection creates a painful, pus-filled lump called an abscess.
  • The Tunnel: Even after an abscess drains or is opened by a doctor, the path the fluid took to reach the skin can stay open. If this path doesn't heal, it becomes a fistula.

Other less common causes include:

  • Bowel conditions like Crohn's disease or ulcerative colitis.
  • Previous surgery or injury in the anal area.
  • Sexually transmitted infections (STIs).
  • Tuberculosis.
  • Radiation treatments for pelvic areas.

Signs It Might Be Time for a Health Checkup

The symptoms of an anal fistula can feel very similar to other issues like piles (hemorrhoids). If you notice any of the following signs, it is important to schedule a health checkup:

  • Constant Pain: Sharp or throbbing pain around the anus that gets worse when you sit, move, or go to the bathroom.
  • Swelling: A tender, warm, or red lump near the anal opening.
  • Unusual Discharge: Pus, blood, or smelly fluid leaking from a small opening near the anus.
  • Skin Irritation: Soreness, redness, or itching around the anus caused by the fluid draining.
  • Repeating Abscesses: Painful lumps that seem to drain and go away, but keep coming back.
  • Fever or Chills: Feeling generally sick or unusually tired, which can be a sign of an active infection.

Getting a Proper Diagnosis

If you are experiencing these symptoms, please don't wait in pain. Booking a doctor consultation at Maria Hospital is the first step toward relief.

Our specialists will gently and privately review your symptoms. A simple physical exam is usually enough to find the fistula. Because these tunnels can sometimes branch out, your doctor might recommend painless imaging tests (like an MRI) to get a clear picture before deciding on a treatment plan. If the area is simply too painful, we can also examine you while you are comfortably asleep.

Effective Treatment at Maria Hospital

Anal fistulas very rarely heal on their own, and home remedies or antibiotics aren't enough to cure the tunnel. Surgery is almost always needed to fix the problem permanently.

The main goal of our surgeons is to cure the fistula while carefully protecting the muscles that control your bathroom habits. Based on your specific diagnosis, your doctor will recommend the safest option for you:

  • Fistulotomy: The most common fix for simple fistulas, where the tunnel is opened up so it can heal flat.
  • Seton Placement: For deeper fistulas, a special soft thread is placed in the tunnel to help it drain and heal slowly without harming the muscle.
  • Advanced and Minimally Invasive Options: We also offer advanced treatments like medical glue, laser sealing, or specialized tissue flaps to close the tunnel gently.

Smooth Recovery and Aftercare

The good news is that most of these treatment procedures are quick, and many patients can go home the very same day. Full healing takes a few weeks. To make your recovery as smooth as possible, your care team will recommend:

  • Soaking in warm water (sitz baths) a few times a day to ease discomfort.
  • Drinking lots of water and eating fiber-rich foods to prevent constipation.
  • Taking mild stool softeners so bathroom trips are easy and pain-free.
  • Wearing a small pad to keep your clothes clean while the area heals completely.
