Piles (Hemorrhoids) Treatment

It is completely normal to feel a bit uncomfortable talking about issues with your bottom, but there is no need to suffer in silence or feel embarrassed. Hemorrhoids, commonly known as piles, are actually a natural part of the human body. We are all born with these small, pillow-like clusters of veins inside and around the lower rectum and anus, which help control bowel movements.

However, they become a medical issue when these veins swell, stretch, or become inflamed—much like varicose veins. Piles are incredibly common, affecting about half of all adults by age 50. Types of Hemorrhoids

Hemorrhoids are classified by where they develop:

  • Internal Hemorrhoids: These form inside the rectum. Because this area lacks pain-sensing nerves, they are usually painless and you cannot feel them. They are grouped into four stages:
    • Grade 1: Stays completely inside the anal canal; may cause mild bleeding.
    • Grade 2: Bulges out during a bowel movement but goes back inside naturally.
    • Grade 3: Bulges out during exertion and must be gently pushed back in with a finger.
    • Grade 4: Stays completely outside the anus and cannot be pushed back in.
  • External Hemorrhoids: These develop under the highly sensitive skin around the outside of the anus. They are often itchy, tender, and uncomfortable.
  • Prolapsed Hemorrhoids: This happens when an internal hemorrhoid stretches down and bulges outside the anal opening.
  • Thrombosed Hemorrhoids: Sometimes, blood pools in an external hemorrhoid and forms a clot. This creates a hard, firm lump (often blue or purple) that can cause sudden, severe pain.

Common Symptoms to Watch For

Many people have piles without any symptoms. When symptoms do appear, they usually include:

  • Painless, bright red blood on your stool, in the toilet bowl, or on toilet paper.
  • Itching, irritation, or a burning sensation around the anus.
  • Achy pain or discomfort, especially when sitting or passing stool.
  • A hard, tender lump near your anus.

What Causes Piles?

Piles develop when too much pressure is placed on the veins in your lower rectum. Common causes and risk factors include:

  • Straining hard during a bowel movement.
  • Sitting on the toilet for a long time.
  • Having chronic constipation or diarrhea.
  • Eating a diet low in dietary fiber.
  • Pregnancy and childbirth (the weight of the baby and the pressure of pushing).
  • Regularly lifting heavy objects or doing strenuous labor.
  • Aging, as tissues supporting the veins naturally weaken over time.
  • Being overweight or obese.

When to See a Doctor

While piles are usually harmless, you should never assume that rectal bleeding is "just a hemorrhoid." Bleeding can also signal more serious conditions, such as colorectal or anal cancer.

A prompt doctor consultation is essential if you experience bleeding for the first time, if your symptoms do not improve after a week of home care, or if you have severe pain. Seek emergency medical care if you experience large amounts of bleeding, feel dizzy, or faint.

Getting a Proper Diagnosis

During a health checkup at Maria Hospital, our doctors will ask about your symptoms and perform a gentle physical examination. They can often identify external hemorrhoids simply by looking at the area. To check for internal hemorrhoids, they may perform a digital rectal exam, which involves gently inserting a lubricated, gloved finger into the rectum to feel for unusual lumps.

For a clearer diagnosis, your doctor may also use an anoscope, proctoscope, or sigmoidoscope—short, lighted tubes with cameras—to view the inside of your anal canal. In some cases, a full colonoscopy may be recommended to rule out other digestive diseases.

Prevention and At-Home Treatments

Most mild hemorrhoid symptoms can be managed at home within a week. Try these simple steps to soothe symptoms and prevent piles from worsening:

  • Eat more fiber: Add fruits, vegetables, beans, and whole grains to your diet, or take an over-the-counter fiber supplement. This softens your stool, so you don't have to strain.
  • Drink plenty of fluids: Drink six to eight glasses of water daily to keep stools soft.
  • Take a "Sitz Bath": Soak your anal area in plain, warm water for 10 to 15 minutes, two or three times a day, to relieve pain, swelling, and itching.
  • Don't ignore the urge: Use the restroom as soon as you feel the urge to poop. Waiting can dry out the stool and make it harder to pass.
  • Use over-the-counter relief: Apply hemorrhoid creams containing hydrocortisone or lidocaine, or use soothing witch hazel pads. Pain relievers like acetaminophen or ibuprofen can also ease discomfort.
  • Avoid straining: Do not push too hard on the toilet or sit for prolonged periods.

Medical and Surgical Treatments

If your piles are large, bleed persistently, or do not respond to home treatments, Maria Hospital offers safe and effective treatment options:

  • Rubber Band Ligation: Tiny rubber bands are placed around the base of an internal hemorrhoid to cut off its blood supply, causing it to shrivel and fall off within a week.
  • Sclerotherapy: A chemical solution is injected into the hemorrhoid to create a scar and shrink the swollen tissue.
  • Coagulation Therapy: Using infrared light, heat, or an electric current, a doctor hardens and shrivels small, bleeding internal hemorrhoids.
  • External Hemorrhoid Thrombectomy: If a painful blood clot has formed within the last 72 hours, a doctor can safely numb the area and remove the clot for immediate relief.
  • Hemorrhoidectomy: A highly effective surgery performed under anesthesia to cut out large external hemorrhoids or severe prolapsed internal hemorrhoids.
  • Stapled Hemorrhoidopexy: A surgeon uses a stapling device to pull prolapsed internal tissue back into place and cut off its blood supply, causing it to shrink.
