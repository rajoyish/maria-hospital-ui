Anal Fissures Treatment

An anal fissure is a small tear or crack in the delicate skin lining the lower rectum and anus. While the tear itself might be tiny, it can cause significant discomfort because this area is highly sensitive. Fissures are very common and can affect people of all ages, from infants to older adults.

Here is everything you need to know about spotting, treating, and preventing anal fissures.

Signs You Might Need a Health Checkup

People often confuse anal fissures with piles (hemorrhoids) because the symptoms are very similar. However, fissures typically show the following signs:

  • Intense, sharp pain during a bowel movement, which can sometimes turn into a deep, burning ache that lasts for hours.
  • Bright red blood visible on your stool, in the toilet bowl, or on the toilet paper.
  • Itching or burning irritation around the anal area.
  • A visible tear or crack in the skin, sometimes with a small lump or "skin tag" next to it.
  • Muscle spasms in the anal ring.

What Causes Anal Fissures?

Most tears happen when the anal canal is stretched beyond its normal capacity. The most common triggers include:

  • Constipation and straining to pass hard or large stools.
  • Frequent or severe diarrhea.
  • Trauma from childbirth.
  • Having overly tight anal muscles, which reduces blood flow and makes the skin more prone to tearing.
  • Less common causes include inflammatory bowel diseases, certain infections, or anal intercourse.

Acute vs. Chronic Fissures

When you first develop a tear, it is called acute. Most acute fissures are like small paper cuts and will heal on their own within one to six weeks using simple home care.

If a fissure fails to heal and lasts longer than six to eight weeks, it is considered chronic. Chronic fissures are usually deeper and require a professional diagnosis and medical treatment to break the cycle of pain and muscle spasms.

Home Remedies and Self-Care

For mild, acute fissures, your main goal is to ease the pain and keep your stool soft. You can often successfully treat the issue at home by:

  • Taking warm "sitz baths": Sit in a shallow tub of warm water for 10 to 20 minutes, two to three times a day. The warm water soothes the area and relaxes the muscles.
  • Eating a high-fiber diet: Aim for plenty of local fruits, vegetables, lentils (dal), and whole grains to naturally soften your stool.
  • Staying hydrated: Drink plenty of water (at least two liters a day).
  • Practicing gentle hygiene: Avoid rough, dry toilet paper. Use a bidet or water to clean yourself gently after using the toilet.
  • Using stool softeners: Over-the-counter stool softeners can make bathroom trips much less painful.

How to Prevent Future Tears

If you have had an anal fissure once, you are more prone to getting them again. The best way to protect yourself is to maintain healthy bathroom habits.

Never ignore the urge to go to the toilet—delaying it makes the stool harder to pass. Additionally, try placing a small step stool under your feet while sitting on the toilet. This puts your body in a natural squatting position, greatly reducing strain.
