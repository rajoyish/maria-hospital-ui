What is Chronic Constipation?

Generally, constipation is defined as having fewer than three bowel movements a week. It is considered "chronic" when these struggles last for three months or more.

Beyond infrequent bathroom trips, you might also experience:

  • Stools that are dry, hard, or lumpy.
  • Pain or excessive straining when trying to use the bathroom.
  • A feeling that you haven't completely emptied your bowels.
  • The need to press on your abdomen to help pass a stool.
  • Constant stomach aches, cramping, or feeling bloated.

Why Does It Happen?

Chronic constipation is rarely caused by just one issue. It is usually the result of a mix of factors:

  • Diet and Lifestyle: A diet low in fiber, not drinking enough water throughout the day, and lacking regular physical activity are the most common culprits.
  • Ignoring the Urge: Holding it in and delaying a trip to the restroom can weaken your body's natural bowel signals over time.
  • Medications: Many common drugs, including certain painkillers, iron supplements, allergy medications, and blood pressure pills, can slow down your digestion.
  • Medical Conditions: Health issues like diabetes, thyroid problems, or Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) are deeply linked to bowel habits.
  • Muscle Coordination: Sometimes, the pelvic floor muscles do not coordinate well to release stool properly, or the colon simply moves food too slowly.

When is it Time for a Doctor Consultation?

While constipation is common, it is crucial not to ignore your body's warning signs. We highly recommend booking a doctor consultation if you notice any of these red flags:

  • Blood in your stool or on the toilet paper.
  • Severe, non-stop stomach pain.
  • Sudden, unexplained weight loss.
  • Vomiting or an inability to pass gas.
  • A sudden worsening of symptoms, especially if you are over 50.

The Risks of Waiting: If left untreated, the excessive straining from chronic constipation can lead to painful complications like hemorrhoids (piles), anal fissures (small skin tears), or impacted stool. Getting a routine health checkup early is the easiest way to prevent these uncomfortable issues.

Expert Diagnosis at Maria Hospital

Getting to the exact root of the problem is essential. During a visit to Maria Hospital, your healthcare provider will gently discuss your medical history, diet, and symptoms.

To ensure an accurate diagnosis, your doctor might recommend:

  • Physical Exams: A basic exam to check for physical blockages or muscle coordination issues.
  • Colonoscopy: A safe procedure using a tiny camera to look inside your colon and rule out other conditions.
  • Transit and Muscle Studies: Specialized tests to measure how quickly food moves through your digestive system and how well your nerves and muscles are working.

Finding Lasting Relief: Your Treatment Options

The good news is that most cases of chronic constipation can be managed with simple lifestyle changes. If those aren't enough, our medical team is here with advanced options.

1. Simple Dietary Changes

  • Add More Fiber: Gradually add more leafy greens, whole grains, and fresh fruits to your meals to naturally soften your stool. Aim for 25 to 38 grams daily.
  • Try Prunes: Prunes and prune juice contain sorbitol, which acts as a fantastic, gentle natural laxative.
  • Stay Hydrated: Drink plenty of water throughout the day. Fiber needs water to do its job!

2. Everyday Lifestyle Adjustments

  • Keep Moving: Regular physical activity, even a daily walk around your neighborhood, gently stimulates your digestive tract.
  • Build a Routine: Try to sit on the toilet at the same time every day—about 15 to 45 minutes after breakfast is usually ideal.
  • Change Your Posture: Place a small step stool under your feet while on the toilet. This simple trick improves your posture and makes passing stool much easier without straining.

3. Medical Therapies

When diet and exercise aren't quite enough, your doctor will tailor a treatment plan for you, which may include:

  • Safe Laxatives or Supplements: Over-the-counter options like fiber supplements or gentle laxatives that draw water into your bowels.
  • Prescription Medications: For stubborn cases, specialized drugs can help increase fluid in the digestive tract.
  • Biofeedback Therapy: A specialized physical therapist can teach you how to properly relax and coordinate your pelvic muscles during a bowel movement.
