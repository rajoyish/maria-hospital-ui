What is a Nosebleed?

A nosebleed (medically called epistaxis) happens when the tiny blood vessels inside your nose break.

  • Front Nosebleeds: Most cases happen in the front part of the nose. This area is very delicate and easy to irritate.
  • Deep Nosebleeds: These occur deep in the back of the nose. They are less common but can be more serious, especially for older adults.

Why Do I Keep Getting Nosebleeds?

If your nose bleeds often, the lining inside might be dry or damaged. Here are the most common causes:

1. Dryness and Weather

  • Dry Air: The air in Nepal can get very dry, especially during winter or when using indoor heaters. This causes the skin inside your nose to crack and bleed.
  • Nasal Sprays: Using allergy or decongestant sprays too often can thin out the nasal tissue.

2. Physical Irritation

  • Nose Picking: This is a common cause in children. It damages the healing skin, making the bleed start all over again.
  • Blowing Too Hard: Forcefully blowing or rubbing your nose can trigger bleeding.

3. Medical Health Factors

  • Infections: Colds, sinus infections, and allergies cause swelling that makes blood vessels burst easily.
  • Medications: Blood-thinning medicines (like aspirin) make it harder for bleeding to stop.
  • Health Conditions: While rare, conditions like high blood pressure or clotting disorders can make nosebleeds last longer. A proper diagnosis is key to ruling these out.

Immediate First Aid: How to Stop a Nosebleed

If your nose starts bleeding, stay calm and follow these 4 steps:

  1. Sit Up and Lean Forward: Do not tilt your head back or lie down. Leaning back causes blood to flow down your throat, which can make you sick.
  2. Pinch the Soft Part: Use your thumb and finger to firmly pinch the soft, lower part of your nose (nostrils). Press against the hard bone in the middle.
  3. Time It: Hold this pressure for 10 to 15 minutes (5 minutes for children). Do not let go to check if it has stopped until the time is fully up.
  4. Stay Cool: Place an ice pack on your nose or the back of your neck to help stop the flow.

How to Prevent Future Nosebleeds

To stop the cycle of bleeding, you need to keep your nose moist and let it heal.

  • Moisturize: Apply a saline spray or a small amount of Vaseline inside your nostrils to prevent cracking.
  • Humidify: Use a humidifier in your bedroom to keep the air moist, especially during Kathmandu's dry winters.

When to Seek Emergency Care

Please visit the emergency department immediately if:

  • Bleeding does not stop after 20 minutes of pressure.
  • You feel dizzy, lightheaded, or very weak.
  • You are on blood-thinning medication.
  • The nosebleed happened after a hit to the face or head.
