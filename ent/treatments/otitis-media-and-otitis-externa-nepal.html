Otitis Media and Otitis Externa Treatment

Ear pain is a common health issue that can ruin your sleep and disrupt your daily routine. However, not all ear pain is the same. Knowing the difference between an outer ear infection (Otitis Externa) and a middle ear infection (Otitis Media) is important because they require very different treatments.

Whether you are suffering from a sudden earache or your child is crying from ear pain, getting the right diagnosis is key. This guide helps you understand the symptoms, causes, and best treatment options available in Kathmandu, Nepal.

1. Otitis Externa (Outer Ear Infection)

What is it?

Often called "Swimmer’s Ear," this is an infection of the ear canal—the tube connecting your outer ear to the eardrum. In Nepal, this often happens during humid weather or after swimming. The skin inside the ear is thin and sensitive, making it easy for bacteria to grow if it stays wet.

Common Causes

  • Moisture: Water trapped in the ear from swimming or high humidity weakens the skin.
  • Trauma: Cleaning ears with cotton swabs (Q-tips), fingernails, or hairpins is a major cause. This causes tiny scratches where bacteria enter.
  • Chemicals: Shampoo, hair spray, or hair dye can irritate the ear canal.
  • Fungus: Sometimes, the infection is fungal, especially if you have used antibiotic drops for too long without a doctor's advice.

Symptoms to Watch For

  • Pain on Touch: The clearest sign is severe pain when you pull your earlobe or push the small bump (tragus) in front of your ear.
  • Itching: Often the first sign before the pain starts.
  • Swelling: The ear might feel "blocked" or muffled.
  • Discharge: You might see fluid draining out, which can be clear or pus-like.

Treatment

Treatment is usually applied directly to the ear.

  • Ear Drops: A doctor consultation is necessary to get the right antibiotic or antifungal drops.
  • Ear Wicks: If the swelling is severe, a doctor at Maria Hospital may insert a tiny sponge (wick) to help the medicine reach inside.
  • Pain Relief: Simple painkillers help manage the discomfort.

2. Otitis Media (Middle Ear Infection)

What is it?

This is an infection behind the eardrum. It is very common in young children in Nepal because their drainage tubes (Eustachian tubes) are smaller and easily blocked.

Common Causes

Unlike outer ear infections, this usually starts inside the body.

  • Colds and Flu: It often follows a respiratory infection, cold, or allergy. Congestion blocks the ear from draining.
  • Fluid Buildup: Fluid gets trapped behind the eardrum and becomes infected by bacteria or viruses.

Symptoms to Watch For

  • Deep Pain: A deep, throbbing pressure inside the head. Unlike outer ear infections, pulling the earlobe usually does not make it hurt more.
  • Fever: High fever is common, especially in children.
  • Hearing Loss: Sounds may seem dull, like you are underwater.
  • Rupture: In severe cases, the eardrum may tear to release the pressure, causing fluid to drain out.

Treatment

  • Wait and See: Since many middle ear infections are viral, they may heal on their own. However, a health checkup is vital to monitor the progress.
  • Oral Antibiotics: If it is a bacterial infection, a doctor will prescribe oral medicine (like Amoxicillin).
  • Medical Procedures: For frequent infections, a specialist might suggest a minor procedure to help the ear drain.

Quick Comparison: How to Tell the Difference

The easiest way to know which infection you have is by checking where the pain comes from.

Feature Otitis Externa (Outer) Otitis Media (Middle)
Common Name Swimmer's Ear Middle Ear Infection
Pain Trigger Pain gets worse when touching/pulling the ear. Deep pain inside; does not change when pulling the ear.
Main Cause Water, scratching (Q-tips). Colds, flu, blocked tubes.
Treatment Ear drops. Oral medication or observation.

Prevention Tips for Healthy Ears

To Prevent Outer Ear Infections:

  • Keep it Dry: Dry your ears gently after showering or swimming.
  • Stop Using Q-Tips: Do not put cotton swabs, pins, or fingers inside your ear canal. This pushes wax deeper and damages the skin.

To Prevent Middle Ear Infections:

  • Hygiene: Wash hands often to avoid catching colds and flu.
  • Avoid Smoke: Keep children away from cigarette smoke or heavy pollution, as this irritates the inner tubes of the ear.
  • Vaccination: Ensure vaccinations are up to date.
