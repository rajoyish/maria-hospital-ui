Secretory Otitis Media (Glue Ear) Treatment

Is your child ignoring you when you call their name? Are they turning the TV volume up too high, or acting more irritable than usual?

They might be suffering from a very common condition known as Secretory Otitis Media, often called "Glue Ear." This happens when fluid builds up inside the ear, even when there is no active infection.

At Maria Hospital in Kathmandu, we understand how worrying hearing issues can be for parents. We are here to help you understand this condition, how to spot it, and the best treatment options available in Nepal.

What is Glue Ear (Secretory Otitis Media)?

The middle ear is the space behind the eardrum. Usually, this space is filled with air, which helps sound travel to the brain. However, with Secretory Otitis Media, this space fills up with fluid.

Unlike a painful ear infection, this fluid is not infected (no pus). It can be thick and sticky like glue, which is why it is called "Glue Ear." Because there is usually no pain or fever, many parents in Nepal don't realize their child has it until they notice behavioral changes.

Why Does Fluid Build Up?

The main cause is a blockage in the tube that connects the ear to the throat (the Eustachian tube). When this tube gets blocked by swelling or mucus, fluid gets trapped.

Common causes include:

  • Recovering from a cold or flu: Congestion often blocks the ears.
  • Previous ear infections: Fluid can stay behind long after the infection is gone.
  • Allergies: Very common in Kathmandu due to dust and pollen.
  • Enlarged Adenoids: Swollen tissue in the throat that blocks drainage.

Signs and Symptoms to Watch For

Since Glue Ear is usually painless, you need to watch for changes in behavior. If you notice these signs, it may be time for a doctor consultation:

  • Hearing difficulties: Your child frequently asks "What?" or needs you to repeat things.
  • Volume issues: They watch cartoons or listen to music at a very high volume.
  • Speech delay: Young children may be slow to start talking or have unclear speech.
  • Balance issues: Clumsiness or unsteadiness when walking.
  • Ear sensation: Older children may complain that their ear feels "full," pops, or rings.

Who is Most at Risk?

While adults can get this, it is most common in children between 6 months and 3 years old. Their ear tubes are smaller and flatter, making it harder for fluid to drain naturally. Risk factors include attending daycare, exposure to cigarette smoke, or bottle-feeding while lying flat.

Diagnosis at Maria Hospital

If you suspect your child has fluid in their ears, a professional health checkup is necessary. At Maria Hospital, our specialists use advanced tools to provide an accurate diagnosis:

  1. Visual Exam: We use a specialized light to look at the eardrum for fluid bubbles or color changes.
  2. Pneumatic Test: We gently puff air into the ear to see if the eardrum moves properly.
  3. Tympanometry: A quick pressure test to confirm if fluid is blocking the drum.
  4. Hearing Test: If the problem has lasted a long time, we check hearing levels to ensure no permanent damage is occurring.

Treatment Options

The good news is that Glue Ear is usually temporary and treatable.

  • Watchful Waiting: In many cases, the fluid clears up on its own within 4 to 6 weeks. We may monitor your child for a few months to see if it resolves naturally.
  • Home Exercises: Older children can try "popping" their ears (gentle breathing out while pinching the nose) to open the tubes.
  • Surgery (Ear Tubes): If the fluid lasts more than 3 months or affects speech and school performance, we may suggest a simple procedure. A tiny tube (grommet) is placed in the ear to drain fluid and let air in. This is a safe, routine procedure.

Note: Antibiotics are usually not effective for this specific condition unless there is also a new active infection.
