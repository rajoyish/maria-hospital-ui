Ear Discharge (Otorrhea) Treatment

What is Ear Discharge?

Ear discharge is any fluid that leaks out of the ear canal. The color of the fluid can tell you a lot about what is happening inside your ear:

  • Earwax (Brown/Orange): This is natural. Your body makes wax to protect the ear. If it gets wet or melts in the heat, it might look like a runny discharge, but it is usually harmless.
  • Pus or Cloudy Fluid (White/Yellow/Green): If the fluid is thick or smells bad, it usually signals an infection. You should seek a diagnosis from a doctor.
  • Clear Watery Fluid: This is often water trapped after a bath or swimming, or sometimes tears that flowed into the ear.
  • Bloody Fluid: This often means a minor scratch inside the ear, perhaps from cleaning it too roughly.

Common Causes of Ear Discharge

Most discharge comes from the ear canal or the middle ear (the space behind your eardrum).

1. Ear Infections

  • Middle Ear Infection: Bacteria or viruses can cause fluid to build up behind the eardrum. If the pressure gets too high, the eardrum may tear (rupture). This releases the fluid (pus) and often stops the pain, but it requires medical treatment.
  • Swimmer’s Ear: If water stays in the ear after swimming or bathing, bacteria can grow. This causes the ear canal to swell and weep fluid.

2. Injury or Trauma

  • Cleaning: Using cotton swabs (Q-tips), matchsticks, or feathers to clean ears is common but dangerous. It can scratch the ear or push wax deeper.
  • Pressure Changes: Flying or diving can sometimes affect the eardrum.

3. Foreign Objects

Children often put small objects like beads, food, or toy parts in their ears. This can lead to infection and discharge.

How to Care for a Leaking Ear at Home

If you notice discharge, follow these steps to keep the ear clean and safe until you can visit Maria Hospital.

The "Wicking" Method (Safe Cleaning)

Do not put anything deep inside the ear. Instead, use this method:

  1. Wash your hands thoroughly.
  2. Twist a piece of soft, absorbent tissue into a pointed "wick" shape.
  3. Gently place the tip into the outer ear canal (do not push deep).
  4. Let it soak up the fluid for a minute, then replace it with a clean one.
  5. Repeat until the tissue comes out dry.

Important Do’s and Don’ts

  • DO keep the ear dry. Avoid swimming. When bathing, be careful not to let water enter the ear.
  • DO NOT use cotton buds (Q-tips) inside the ear. They can damage the eardrum.
  • DO NOT block the ear with cotton wool unless a doctor tells you to.
  • DO NOT pour oil or water into the ear if you think the eardrum might be burst.

When to See a Doctor

Some minor issues heal on their own, but you should visit Maria Hospital for a health checkup if you notice these warning signs:

  • Duration: The discharge lasts longer than 3 to 5 days.
  • Smell/Color: The fluid is yellow, green, or has a bad smell.
  • Pain: You have fever, severe pain, or redness/swelling behind the ear.
  • Health Conditions: The patient has diabetes or a weak immune system.
  • Hearing: You notice hearing loss.

Go to the hospital immediately if:

  • The discharge happens after a head injury.
  • There is clear or bloody fluid leaking after a blow to the head.
  • There is difficulty speaking, swallowing, or seeing.
