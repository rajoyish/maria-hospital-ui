Perforated/Ruptured Eardrum Treatment

A perforated eardrum—commonly known as a ruptured eardrum—is a small hole or tear in the thin tissue separating your outer ear from your middle ear.

Think of your eardrum like the skin of a drum. It has two vital jobs:

  • It vibrates to help you hear clearly.
  • It acts as a shield to keep water, bacteria, and dust out of your inner ear.

If this barrier breaks, it can affect your hearing and lead to infections. At Maria Hospital in Kathmandu, Nepal, we provide expert doctor consultation and care to help your ear heal safely.

Common Signs and Symptoms

You might not always know right away that your eardrum has torn. However, if you notice these signs, it is time for a health checkup:

  • Sudden Ear Pain: A sharp pain that may subside quickly (often because the pressure from infection has been released).
  • Drainage: You might see clear fluid, pus, or even a little blood leaking from the ear.
  • Hearing Issues: Sounds may feel muffled, or you might have trouble hearing clearly.
  • Ringing Sound (Tinnitus): A buzzing or humming noise in the ear.
  • Dizziness: You may feel like the room is spinning (vertigo) or feel nauseous.

Why Does This Happen?

A ruptured eardrum can happen to anyone, from children to adults. The most common causes we see include:

  • Middle Ear Infections: This is the #1 cause. Infection causes fluid to build up, creating pressure that eventually bursts the eardrum to release the fluid.
  • Cotton Swabs & Objects: Cleaning your ears with cotton buds, hairpins, or matchsticks is dangerous. These can easily poke a hole in the delicate membrane.
  • Pressure Changes: Rapid changes in air pressure—like flying on a plane or driving through high hills in Nepal—can stress the ear.
  • Loud Noises: Extremely loud sounds, such as blasts or very loud music, can damage the ear.

Diagnosis at Maria Hospital

To find out if you have a rupture, you need a proper diagnosis. When you visit Maria Hospital, our doctors will look inside your ear using a special light called an otoscope.

If needed, we may recommend further tests:

  • Audiometry: A simple test to measure your hearing levels.
  • Lab Tests: If there is discharge, we check for bacteria to prescribe the right medicine.

Treatment Options: Will I Need Surgery?

The good news is that most ruptured eardrums heal on their own within a few weeks without surgery. Our goal is to manage the pain and prevent infection while your body heals.

1. Non-Surgical Care (Most Common)

  • Observation: We will monitor your progress to ensure the hole is closing.
  • Medication: We may prescribe antibiotic drops or pills if there is an infection. Pain relievers can help you feel comfortable.
  • Keeping it Dry: You must keep water out of your ear to prevent complications.

2. Procedures and Surgery

If the hole is large or doesn't heal after two months, we may suggest:

  • Eardrum Patch: A simple procedure where the doctor places a small paper patch over the hole to help tissue grow across it.
  • Tympanoplasty (Surgery): For holes that won't close, a surgeon grafts a tiny piece of your own tissue to close the hole permanently.

Home Care: How to Protect Your Ear

While recovering at home, follow these simple tips:

  • Keep it Dry: Avoid swimming. When showering, place a cotton ball coated in Vaseline (petroleum jelly) in your outer ear to seal out water.
  • Stop Cleaning Inside: Do not put cotton swabs or oil drops inside your ear unless your doctor tells you to.
  • Be Gentle: Avoid blowing your nose hard, as the pressure can damage the healing ear.
  • Travel Care: If possible, avoid flying. If you must travel, chew gum during takeoff and landing.

When to Visit Maria Hospital

Don't ignore ear problems. Complications like chronic infection or permanent hearing loss can occur if left untreated. Please book a doctor consultation immediately if:

  • You have sudden hearing loss or severe pain.
  • You see pus or blood coming from your ear.
  • You feel very dizzy or off-balance.
  • Your symptoms don't get better after a few weeks.
