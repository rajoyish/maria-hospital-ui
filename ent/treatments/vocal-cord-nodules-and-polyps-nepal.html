What Are Vocal Cords?

Your vocal cords are two flexible muscle bands inside your voice box. When you breathe, they stay open. When you speak or sing, air from your lungs gently pushes them together. They vibrate against each other to create the sound of your voice.

Sometimes, if you strain your voice too much, small, non-cancerous growths can form on these cords. The two most common types are vocal cord nodules and vocal cord polyps.

Nodules vs. Polyps: What is the Difference?

Both conditions cause similar voice problems, but they look and develop differently:

  • Vocal Cord Nodules: Think of nodules like the hard calluses you get on your hands from heavy work. They usually grow in pairs (one on each cord). Often called "singer's nodes" or "teacher's nodules," they are common in women, children, and professionals who talk a lot.
  • Vocal Cord Polyps: Polyps are softer, looking more like a small, fluid-filled blister. They are usually larger, reddish in color, and often appear on just one vocal cord. Polyps are more common in men.

What Causes Voice Strain?

The main cause of both nodules and polyps is overusing, misusing, or straining your voice. Common causes include:

  • Talking loudly, shouting, or screaming.
  • Singing improperly or for long periods without resting.
  • Frequent coughing or clearing your throat.
  • Speaking too much when your throat is already sore from a cold.

Other everyday factors can also irritate your vocal cords, including smoking, heavy caffeine or alcohol use, allergies, and acid reflux.

Signs You Need a Health Checkup

How do you know it is time to seek help? Look out for these common symptoms that disrupt your daily life:

  • A hoarse, raspy, or breathy voice that will not go away.
  • Your voice feels exhausted after just a short conversation.
  • Trouble hitting high notes when singing.
  • Your voice breaks or cracks unpredictably.
  • A feeling like there is a "lump" stuck in your throat.
  • Pain in your neck or ear when speaking.

Highly Effective Treatment Options

The best news is that these conditions are not cancer, and they are very treatable. Your personalized treatment plan may include:

  • Voice Rest and Care: Giving your voice a break and drinking plenty of water. If allergies or acid reflux are causing the irritation, your doctor will provide medication to manage them.
  • Voice Therapy: Working with a specialist to learn safer ways to speak and breathe. This simple therapy often shrinks nodules completely!
  • Minor Surgery: If a polyp or a large nodule does not go away with rest, our expert surgeons can carefully remove it. This is a quick procedure with no cuts on your neck, allowing you to go home the same day.

Tips to Protect Your Voice

Keep your voice healthy and strong with these simple daily habits:

  • Drink plenty of water: Keep your throat moist throughout the day.
  • Avoid vocal extremes: Try not to yell, scream, or talk for hours without a break.
  • Use a microphone: If you are teaching or speaking to a crowd, use a mic to avoid straining.
  • Do not smoke: Cigarette smoke is highly damaging to the vocal cords.
  • Warm up: Always prep your voice before heavy singing or public speaking.
