Loss or Decreased Sense of Smell (Anosmia) Treatment

Have you noticed that your morning coffee doesn’t smell as strong as it used to? Or perhaps food has started to taste bland? You might be experiencing Anosmia, which is the medical term for the loss of smell.

At Maria Hospital in Kathmandu, Nepal, we understand that losing this sense can be worrying. It affects how you taste food and can even stop you from sensing danger, like smoke or gas leaks. This article explains why this happens and how our specialists can help you get back to normal.

What is Anosmia?

Anosmia is when you completely lose your sense of smell. If you can still smell a little bit, but not as well as before, it is called hyposmia.

Because smell and taste work together, many people think they have lost their sense of taste. In reality, your tongue can still taste sweet or salty things, but without smell, you miss out on the rich flavors.

Why Did I Lose My Smell?

Loss of smell can be temporary or permanent. Here are the most common reasons:

  • Nasal Congestion & Infections: This is the most common cause. The common cold, flu, or sinus infections (sinusitis) can block the nose. Allergies, which are common in dusty seasons in Nepal, can also cause swelling that hides scents.
  • Nasal Polyps: These are soft, non-cancerous growths inside the nose that block airflow.
  • COVID-19: Many people lose their smell during COVID-19, even without a stuffy nose. Fortunately, for most people, the sense of smell returns as they recover.
  • Head Injuries: Accidents or falls can damage the nerves that send smell signals to the brain.
  • Aging: It is natural for our senses to fade slightly after age 60.

Signs You Should Watch For

You might need a health checkup if you notice these signs:

  • Familiar smells (like spices or perfume) disappear.
  • Food tastes "flat" or strange.
  • Phantosmia: You smell things that aren't there (like smoke).
  • Parosmia: Pleasant smells suddenly seem bad or foul to you.

  • Medical History: We review your recent illnesses and medications.

  • Smell Tests: Simple tests to see which scents you can detect.
  • Advanced Scans: If needed, we use imaging to check your sinuses thoroughly.

Available Treatments

The right treatment depends on what is causing the problem.

  • Medications: Steroid sprays or antibiotics can help clear up infections and allergies.
  • Smell Training: This is a natural therapy where you practice smelling strong scents (like lemon, rose, or clove) twice a day to "retrain" your brain.
  • Surgery: If polyps are blocking your nose, a minor procedure might be needed to clear the airway.

Important Safety Tips

Living without smell means you lose an important warning system. To stay safe at home:

  • Install Detectors: Use smoke and gas detectors, as you won't be able to smell smoke or leaks.
  • Check Expiry Dates: Don't rely on the "sniff test" for food. Read labels carefully to avoid eating spoiled food.
  • Kitchen Safety: Be extra careful with gas stoves. Ensure they are turned off completely after cooking.

When to See a Doctor

Don’t wait if your symptoms persist. You should book a consultation if your loss of smell is sudden, unexplained, or lasts long after a flu has cleared. Early diagnosis leads to better recovery.
