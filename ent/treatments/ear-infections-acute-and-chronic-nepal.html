Ear Infections (Acute and Chronic) Treatment

Is your child crying uncontrollably, or are you feeling a sudden, sharp pain in your ear? You might be dealing with an ear infection. While common, ear pain can be worrying.

At Maria Hospital, we want to help you understand what causes these infections and how to treat them. Whether you are in Kathmandu or surrounding areas in Nepal, knowing when to seek a doctor consultation is the first step toward feeling better.

What Is an Ear Infection?

An ear infection (medically called otitis media) happens when germs—like a virus or bacteria—get trapped in the air-filled space behind the eardrum. This is called the middle ear.

When this area gets infected, it swells and fills with fluid. This pressure causes pain and can affect hearing. While this is one of the most common reasons parents book a health checkup for their children, it can happen to adults too.

Types of Ear Infections: Short-term vs. Long-term

Doctors usually divide ear infections into two categories:

  • Acute Ear Infections: These start quickly and are often very painful. Most clear up within a few days, but the pain requires attention.
  • Chronic Ear Infections: These last a long time (over three months) or keep coming back. While less painful, they can be dangerous because they may cause permanent hearing damage if left untreated.

Why Do They Happen?

Your ear is connected to your throat by a small channel called the Eustachian tube. This tube drains fluid and keeps air flowing. If it gets blocked, fluid builds up and gets infected.

Common triggers in Nepal include:

  • Common colds, the flu, or sinus infections.
  • Allergies caused by dust, pollen, or pollution in the Kathmandu valley.
  • Exposure to cigarette smoke.

Why Are Children More at Risk?

You may notice that children get sick with ear infections much more often than adults. This is because their Eustachian tubes are smaller and lay more flat, making it harder for fluid to drain. Also, children’s immune systems are still learning how to fight off germs.

Signs and Symptoms to Watch For

Symptoms look different depending on age. If you notice these signs, it may be time for a diagnosis at Maria Hospital.

In Babies and Young Children:

  • Crying more than usual and acting irritable.
  • Pulling, tugging, or rubbing the ear.
  • High fever (100.5°F to 104°F).
  • Refusing to eat or trouble sleeping.
  • Fluid draining from the ear.

In Older Children and Adults:

  • Sharp ear pain or a dull ache.
  • A feeling of "fullness" or pressure in the ear.
  • Muffled hearing (like you are underwater).
  • Nausea or dizziness.

How We Diagnose and Treat Ear Infections

When you visit us for a doctor consultation, our specialists use a lighted tool called an otoscope to look inside the ear. We check for redness, fluid, or pressure behind the eardrum.

Treatment Options

  • Home Care: Many infections heal on their own. We may suggest warm compresses, rest, and pain relievers like acetaminophen (Paracetamol) or ibuprofen.
  • Antibiotics: These are only used if the infection is caused by bacteria. Antibiotics do not work on viruses. A doctor must decide if they are necessary.
  • Ear Tubes (Surgery): For children with chronic infections that affect hearing, a small procedure to place tubes in the ear can help drain fluid.

Prevention Tips for Healthy Ears

You can lower the risk of infection for your family with these simple habits:

  • Vaccinate: Keep children up to date on flu and other vaccines.
  • Hygiene: Wash hands often to stop the spread of colds.
  • Avoid Smoke: Keep your home smoke-free.
  • Breastfeeding: Breast milk contains antibodies that fight infection.
  • Feeding Position: If bottle-feeding, hold the baby upright so milk doesn't flow back into the ear tubes.

When to Visit Maria Hospital

While some earaches pass, others need professional medical attention to prevent complications like hearing loss. Please visit Maria Hospital in Kathmandu immediately if:

  • Your child is under 6 months old and seems sick.
  • Fever is higher than 102°F or pain is severe.
  • You see pus or bloody fluid coming from the ear.
  • Symptoms do not improve after 2 to 3 days.
