Oral Ulcers and Oral Lesions Treatment

Getting a painful sore or strange bump in your mouth is very common. While most mouth sores are completely harmless and heal on their own, they can make eating, drinking, and talking uncomfortable.

What are Oral Lesions and Ulcers?

Oral Lesion:
This is a general medical term for any abnormal change in your mouth, such as a lump, blister, swelling, or a strange red or white patch.
Oral Ulcer:
This is a specific type of sore that happens when the top layer of skin inside your mouth breaks away. This exposes sensitive tissue underneath, which is why ulcers usually sting or burn—especially when eating spicy or hot foods.

Common Types of Mouth Sores

1. Canker Sores

These are the most common mouth ulcers. They are small, round sores with a white or yellow center and a red border.

  • Location: Usually inside the cheeks, lips, or under the tongue.
  • Healing: They are not contagious and typically heal in 7 to 14 days without scarring.

2. Cold Sores (Fever Blisters)

Unlike canker sores, cold sores are caused by a virus and are highly contagious.

  • Symptoms: You might feel a tingling or burning sensation a day before a fluid-filled blister appears.
  • Location: They usually form on the lips but can occasionally appear on the gums.

3. Bites and Burns

Sometimes, mouth sores are just accidental injuries.

  • Causes: Accidentally biting your cheek, brushing too aggressively, or burning your mouth on very hot tea or food.
  • Healing: Once the area is protected from further irritation, these heal quickly.

4. Oral Thrush

This is a fungal infection that causes white, cottage-cheese-like patches on your tongue or inner cheeks. It is more common in people taking antibiotics, those with dentures, or individuals with weaker immune systems.

5. Persistent Red or White Patches

If you notice white, leathery patches or velvety red spots that do not scrape off, they need attention. While often harmless, they can sometimes be a sign of precancerous changes.

Vitamins and Your Mouth's Health

Did you know that your daily diet affects the skin inside your mouth? A lack of essential vitamins is a common cause of frequent mouth sores.

  • Vitamins B12 and Folate: Essential for repairing cells. Low levels can lead to burning sensations and frequent, painful ulcers.
  • Vitamin C: Crucial for healing. A deficiency can cause swollen, bleeding gums and make it harder for ulcers to heal.

If you get sores frequently, booking a health checkup at Maria Hospital can help. A simple blood test can provide a clear diagnosis so you can get the right vitamin supplements.

Simple Home Care and Treatment

For minor sores, bites, or burns, you can usually manage the discomfort at home:

  • Diet: Avoid hot, spicy, salty, or acidic foods that can sting the sore. Stick to soft, mild foods.
  • Hygiene: Keep your mouth clean. Gently brush your teeth and use warm saltwater rinses to soothe the area.
  • Pain Relief: Pharmacy gels can help block the pain while the sore heals.

For viral or fungal infections, specific medical treatment like antiviral creams or antifungal mouthwashes may be required to clear the infection.

🚩 Red Flags: When to See a Doctor

Most mouth sores are nothing to worry about. However, early detection is the best tool for catching serious conditions, including oral cancer. Please schedule an urgent doctor consultation if you notice any of the following:

  • The 2-3 Week Rule: Any sore, bump, or patch that does not heal or go away after 2 to 3 weeks must be evaluated by a professional.
  • Swollen Glands: A mouth sore accompanied by a hard, swollen lump in your neck.
  • Difficulty Swallowing: Pain or trouble when you try to swallow food or liquids.
  • Unusual Changes: A sore that is growing quickly, bleeds easily, or feels unusually hard to the touch.
