Enlarged Adenoids Treatment

Watching your child struggle with breathing, sleeping, or constant ear infections is stressful for any parent. At Maria Hospital in Kathmandu, Nepal, we want you to know that these issues are often caused by a common condition called Enlarged Adenoids.

We are here to help. This simple article explains what adenoids are, why they cause problems, and how our expert team can help your family breathe easier.

What Are Adenoids?

Adenoids are small patches of tissue hidden high up in the throat, just behind the nose. You cannot see them like you can see tonsils.

They are part of your child’s immune system. Their main job is to act like a "filter" to trap germs and fight infections.

Key Fact: Adenoids are largest in children aged 2 to 6. They usually shrink on their own by the teenage years.

Why Do They Get Swollen?

Adenoids naturally swell when fighting a cold or infection. Usually, they shrink back down. However, sometimes they stay swollen due to:

  • Recurring infections.
  • Allergies or pollution.
  • Genetics (some children are just born with larger ones).

Common Signs and Symptoms

Since you cannot see adenoids, you must watch for the symptoms. If your child has enlarged adenoids, they might block the air passage behind the nose. Look for these signs:

  • Mouth Breathing: Your child breathes through their mouth day and night because their nose feels blocked.
  • Loud Snoring: They may snore loudly or have pauses in breathing during sleep (Sleep Apnea), leading to tiredness during the day.
  • Ear Problems: Frequent ear infections or "glue ear" (fluid buildup) can happen, sometimes affecting hearing.
  • Nasal Voice: They sound like they have a permanent cold.

Diagnosis and Health Checkup at Maria Hospital

If you suspect your child has this condition, a professional doctor consultation is the first step. Because adenoids are hidden, you cannot diagnose them at home.

At Maria Hospital, our specialists use precise methods for accurate diagnosis:

  • Physical Exam: We listen to your concerns about snoring and breathing.
  • Nasopharyngoscopy: A quick, gentle look at the adenoids using a tiny camera tube.
  • X-rays: To see the size of the adenoids and the airway blockage.

Effective Treatment Options

Not every child needs surgery. Our goal is to find the right treatment plan for your child’s specific needs.

1. Watchful Waiting

For mild cases, we may simply monitor the condition during regular health checkups, as adenoids often shrink with age.

2. Medications

  • Nasal Sprays: To reduce swelling and inflammation.
  • Antibiotics: To treat bacterial infections.
  • Allergy Management: Medicine to help if allergies are the cause.

3. Surgery (Adenoidectomy)

If the symptoms are severe and affect your child’s sleep or hearing, we may recommend a simple surgery to remove the adenoids.

  • Quick Procedure: It takes only 20–30 minutes under general anesthesia.
  • No Scars: The surgery is done through the mouth, so there are no cuts on the skin.
  • Fast Recovery: Most children go home the same day and recover within a week.
