What is a Bartholin Cyst?

The Bartholin glands are two small, pea-sized organs located just under the skin at the opening of the vagina. You normally cannot see or feel them. Their job is to release fluid that keeps the area moist and comfortable.

This fluid travels through tiny tubes called ducts. If a duct gets blocked, the fluid gets trapped inside. It then swells into a fluid-filled sac, which is called a Bartholin cyst. These can be as small as a pea or grow as large as a golf ball.

Symptoms: What Does it Feel Like?

Many small Bartholin cysts are painless and might go completely unnoticed. However, if the cyst grows, you might experience:

  • A painless lump or swelling near the vaginal opening (usually on one side).
  • A feeling of fullness or pressure in the pelvic area.
  • Discomfort or pain when sitting, walking, or during intimacy.

When it Becomes an Abscess (Infection)

If bacteria get trapped inside the blocked fluid, the cyst can become infected and form a painful pocket of pus, known as an abscess. Abscesses are very common and require a doctor consultation. Symptoms of an infection include:

  • Intense pain and tenderness that makes sitting or walking very difficult.
  • Redness, swelling, and a hot sensation around the lump.
  • Drainage of foul-smelling fluid or pus.
  • Fever and chills.

What Causes a Bartholin Cyst?

The exact reason a duct blocks isn't always clear, but several factors can increase your risk:

  • Bacterial Infections: Common bacteria (like E. coli) or sexually transmitted infections (STIs) can cause swelling that blocks the duct.
  • Physical Trauma: An injury to the area or trauma during childbirth can cause scarring that leads to a blockage.
  • Thickened Mucus: Sometimes, the fluid simply becomes too thick and clogs the opening.
  • Hygiene and Clothing: Wearing tight, synthetic underwear that traps sweat or having poor genital hygiene can increase your risk.

Note: These cysts most commonly affect women between the ages of 20 and 40. They are less common after menopause.

Treatment Options for Fast Relief

Your treatment plan will depend on the size of the cyst, your pain level, and whether there is an infection. Your doctor will carefully explain the best option for you:

  • Home Remedies: For small, mildly uncomfortable cysts, soaking in a warm, shallow bath for 10 to 15 minutes a few times a day can help the cyst drain naturally. Over-the-counter pain medicine can also help.
  • Antibiotics: If your cyst has turned into a painful abscess, your doctor will likely prescribe antibiotics to quickly clear the infection.
  • Surgical Drainage: For a large or very painful abscess, a doctor can numb the area and make a tiny cut to drain the fluid safely. A tiny, soft tube might be left in place for a few weeks to keep the duct open while it heals completely.
  • Marsupialization: If cysts keep coming back, a minor surgical procedure can be done to create a permanent, small opening that allows the gland to drain continuously.
  • Gland Removal: In very rare cases where other treatments haven't worked, the entire gland can be safely removed.

Prevention and When to See a Doctor

Practicing good hygiene, wearing breathable cotton underwear, and practicing safe sex can help lower your risk of the infections that cause these blockages.

Do not wait if you are in pain. You should contact a healthcare provider immediately if a lump does not improve after a few days of warm baths, if it becomes highly painful, or if you develop a fever.
