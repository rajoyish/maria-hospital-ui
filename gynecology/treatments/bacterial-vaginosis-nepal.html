Bacterial Vaginosis (BV) Treatment

Vaginal health is a vital part of your overall well-being. It is completely normal to experience occasional changes, but if things feel or smell a little off, it is important to address them. At Maria Hospital in Kathmandu, Nepal, we frequently see women for a very common condition called Bacterial Vaginosis (BV).

BV is the most frequent vaginal infection for women ages 15 to 44. While it can be uncomfortable and frustrating, it is highly treatable. Here is everything you need to know about BV, what to look out for, and how a quick doctor consultation can help you find relief.

What is Bacterial Vaginosis?

Every healthy vagina is home to a balanced mix of "good" and "bad" bacteria. The good bacteria keep your vagina slightly acidic and healthy. BV happens when this natural balance is thrown off, allowing the bad bacteria to grow too quickly and overpower the good ones.

While BV is not a sexually transmitted infection (STI), it is strongly linked to sexual activity. Having sex can change your natural chemistry and increase your chances of developing this bacterial imbalance.

Common Causes and Risk Factors

Anything that changes the natural environment of your vagina can trigger BV. You may have a higher risk of developing it if you:

  • Have new or multiple sex partners: This can alter the bacterial environment in your vagina, especially without condoms.
  • Use douches or scented products: Washing the inside of your vagina with perfumed soaps or douches washes away your good bacteria and causes inflammation.
  • Wear tight or unbreathable clothing: Tight yoga pants or nylon underwear can trap moisture, encouraging bad bacteria to grow.
  • Smoke cigarettes: Smoking is a known trigger for bacterial imbalances.
  • Are pregnant or use an IUD: Hormonal changes during pregnancy or using an intrauterine device (IUD) can make you more susceptible.

Symptoms: What to Look Out For

Surprisingly, many women with BV do not experience any symptoms at all. If you do, the most common signs include:

  • A thin, watery discharge that may be off-white, gray, or slightly green.
  • A strong, "fishy" odor, which often gets stronger after sex or during your period.
  • A mild burning sensation when you urinate.
  • Occasional itching or irritation around the outside of the vagina.

Note: BV is often confused with a yeast infection. However, yeast infections usually cause a thick, white, cottage-cheese-like discharge and severe itching, without the strong odor.

Why You Shouldn't Ignore BV

While BV is a mild infection, getting a proper diagnosis is important. If left untreated, BV can make you vulnerable to more serious health complications, including:

  • Increased risk of STIs: BV lowers your natural defenses, making it easier to catch infections like chlamydia or gonorrhea if exposed.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID): BV can lead to infections in your reproductive organs, which can cause difficulties getting pregnant later.
  • Pregnancy Complications: Untreated BV during pregnancy can increase the risk of premature birth.

Diagnosis and Treatment at Maria Hospital

Because BV shares symptoms with other infections, it is best to avoid over-the-counter home remedies, which can often make the irritation worse. Instead, schedule a health checkup with a gynecologist.

Your doctor can easily diagnose BV by:

  • Discussing your symptoms and medical history.
  • Performing a gentle pelvic exam to check for signs of infection.
  • Taking a quick swab of your vaginal fluid to check for BV-causing bacteria.

The treatment for BV is straightforward. Your doctor will likely prescribe antibiotics in the form of oral pills or a vaginal cream. It is crucial to finish all your medication exactly as prescribed, even if your symptoms disappear quickly, to prevent the infection from returning.

Tips to Prevent BV

While you cannot entirely prevent BV, you can take simple steps to keep your body healthy and reduce your risk:

  • Say no to douching: Let your vagina clean itself. Use only plain water or mild, unscented soap on the outside of your genital area.
  • Wear breathable fabrics: Choose cotton underwear to wick away moisture.
  • Practice safe sex: Use condoms and properly clean sex toys after every use.
  • Wipe front to back: Always wipe from front to back after using the toilet to prevent transferring bacteria.
