Cervical Erosion (Ectropion) Treatment

Hearing the words "cervical erosion" or "cervical ectropion" can understandably feel alarming. But rest assured, despite the unsettling name, this is a very common and completely harmless condition.

Here is a simple breakdown of what cervical ectropion is, why it happens, and how a routine doctor consultation can help you manage it.

What is Cervical Ectropion?

To understand this condition, it helps to know a little about your cervix (the lower part of the womb that opens into the vagina). The cervix is made up of two types of cells:

  • Inside: Soft, delicate cells that produce mucus.
  • Outside: Harder, firmer protective cells.

Cervical ectropion simply means that the soft cells from the inside have spread outward, becoming visible on the outer surface of the cervix. Because these inner cells are delicate and red, they can make the cervix look inflamed or "eroded." However, the cervix is not actually damaged—which is why the old medical term "cervical erosion" is a bit misleading!

What Causes It?

Cervical ectropion is a completely natural reaction to high levels of the hormone estrogen. Because it is linked to hormones, it is most frequently seen in:

  • Teenagers and young women experiencing puberty.
  • Pregnant women.
  • Women taking estrogen-based birth control, such as the combined pill.

Note: It is rarely seen in women who have reached menopause because estrogen levels naturally drop during that time.

What are the Symptoms?

Most women do not experience any symptoms at all and only discover they have ectropion during a routine health checkup or Pap smear.

However, because those soft inner cells are sensitive, they can sometimes become irritated. If you do have symptoms, they may include:

  • Increased vaginal discharge (usually clear and odorless).
  • Spotting or bleeding during or after sex.
  • Light spotting between your regular menstrual periods.
  • Mild discomfort or pain during or after sex.

Important Reassurance: Cervical ectropion is completely benign. It is not an infection, it does not affect your ability to have children, and it does not lead to cervical cancer.

Getting a Diagnosis

A quick and simple diagnosis at Maria Hospital can give you peace of mind. Your doctor can easily identify cervical ectropion during a standard pelvic exam.

Because symptoms like unusual bleeding can sometimes mimic other issues, your doctor may recommend a few routine tests just to be safe:

  • Pap smear: To check the health of your cervical cells.
  • Swabs: To rule out any common infections.
  • Colposcopy: A gentle exam using a bright magnifying light to get a closer look at the tissue.

Do I Need Treatment?

If you don't have any bothersome symptoms, you don't need any treatment. The condition often resolves naturally as you age, after childbirth, or if you stop taking combined birth control pills.

However, if heavy discharge or bleeding is affecting your daily comfort, we offer highly effective, quick treatments right here at our hospital in Kathmandu:

  • Changing Birth Control: Switching to a non-hormonal method or a progesterone-only pill can often clear up the issue.
  • Cryotherapy: Using a cold probe to safely freeze and remove the delicate cells.
  • Diathermy: Using a tiny, heated instrument to gently seal the cells.
  • Silver Nitrate: Applying a specialized medical stick to stop the cells from bleeding.

After Treatment:

These procedures take just a few minutes. Afterward, it is completely normal to experience mild, period-like cramps and a watery discharge for a few weeks. To heal properly, you will just need to avoid swimming, using tampons, and having sex for about four weeks.

When to Contact a Doctor

While cervical ectropion is harmless, any unusual changes in your body should always be evaluated by a professional. Please seek medical advice if you experience:

  • Bleeding between periods or after intercourse.
  • Foul-smelling, yellow, or green vaginal discharge.
  • Severe pelvic pain.
  • Heavy bleeding or a high fever after a treatment procedure.
