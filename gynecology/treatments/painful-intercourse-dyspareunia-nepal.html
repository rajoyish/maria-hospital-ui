Painful Intercourse (Dyspareunia) Treatment

Experiencing pain during intimacy can be distressing, but please know that you are not alone. Painful intercourse, medically known as dyspareunia, is a highly common condition affecting nearly 3 out of 4 women at some point in their lives.

What Does Dyspareunia Feel Like?

Dyspareunia is defined as persistent or recurring genital pain that happens just before, during, or after sex. Doctors generally classify this pain based on where and when you feel it:

  • Superficial (Entry) Pain: Felt at the entrance of the vagina when penetration is first attempted.
  • Deep Pain: Felt deeper inside the pelvis or lower back, usually occurring during deeper penetration.
  • Primary Dyspareunia: Pain that has been present since you first became sexually active.
  • Secondary Dyspareunia: Pain that develops later in life after a period of previously pain-free intimacy.

Common signs include a sharp or burning sensation, deep pelvic cramping, or a throbbing ache that lasts for hours after sex.

Common Causes of Painful Intercourse

Pain during sex can stem from a mix of physical and emotional factors.

Causes of Entry (Superficial) Pain

  • Vaginal Dryness: Often caused by a lack of natural lubrication, hormonal changes during menopause, breastfeeding, or certain medications.
  • Vaginismus: An involuntary spasming and tightening of the pelvic floor muscles, making penetration extremely painful.
  • Infections: Sexually transmitted infections (STIs) , yeast infections , or urinary tract infections (UTIs) that cause inflammation.
  • Skin Conditions: Eczema, allergic reactions, or irritations affecting the delicate skin of the vulva.
  • Injury or Trauma: Scarring from childbirth, previous pelvic surgery, or accidents.

Causes of Deep Pelvic Pain

  • Endometriosis: A condition where tissue similar to the uterine lining grows outside the uterus, causing chronic pain.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) : An infection that inflames tissues deep within the reproductive organs.
  • Fibroids or Cysts: Non-cancerous growths on the uterus or fluid-filled sacs on the ovaries.

Emotional and Psychological Causes

Emotions play a massive role in physical comfort. Stress, anxiety, depression, and body image concerns can prevent the body from naturally relaxing and lubricating. Additionally, distress from past trauma can cause involuntary muscle tightening, leading directly to physical pain.

Getting a Proper Diagnosis

Finding relief starts with a simple doctor consultation. At Maria Hospital, our specialists will start with a compassionate, non-judgmental conversation about your health and medical history.

To pinpoint the exact cause, your doctor may perform a gentle pelvic exam to look for signs of infection or skin irritation. Depending on what they find, they may also recommend a quick swab test or an ultrasound to safely examine your deeper pelvic organs. This accurate diagnosis is the most important step toward healing.

Effective Treatment Options

Because dyspareunia has many different causes, your treatment plan will be completely personalized to your specific needs. The good news is that most underlying causes are highly treatable.

  • Lifestyle and Home Care: Using water-based lubricants, taking warm baths to relax muscles, and taking time for intimacy can greatly reduce friction and discomfort.
  • Medications: Antibiotics or antifungal medicines easily clear up infections. For hormonal changes, vaginal estrogen creams or tablets can help restore tissue moisture and health.
  • Pelvic Floor Therapy: A specialized physical therapist can teach you gentle exercises to relieve the muscle spasms and tension associated with vaginismus.
  • Counseling: Speaking with a therapist can be incredibly helpful for reducing anxiety, resolving relationship stress, or healing from past trauma.
  • Surgery: In rare instances, minimally invasive surgery may be recommended to treat deeper issues like severe endometriosis or fibroids.
