Painful Periods / Cramps (Dysmenorrhea) Treatment

Getting your menstrual period often comes with a bit of discomfort. But if severe pain and cramping are stopping you from going to work, attending college, or enjoying your daily life, you might have a medical condition called dysmenorrhea.

At Maria Hospital, located right here in Kathmandu, Nepal, we believe no woman should have to suffer through extreme period pain in silence. Dysmenorrhea is incredibly common, affecting up to 90% of women and teens. Here is a simple guide to understanding painful periods, and how getting the right diagnosis and treatment can help you take your life back.

Understanding the Types of Period Pain

There are two main types of dysmenorrhea:

  • Primary Dysmenorrhea: This is the most common type. It involves painful cramps without any underlying disease, usually starting a year or two after your first period. The good news? It often naturally improves as you get older or after childbirth.
  • Secondary Dysmenorrhea: This pain is caused by an underlying health issue in your reproductive organs. It usually starts later in life, and the pain often lasts much longer than normal cramps.

What Causes the Pain?

During your period, your uterus squeezes (contracts) to shed its lining. Natural chemicals in your body called prostaglandins trigger this process. If you have higher levels of these chemicals, the contractions are stronger, which can temporarily cut off oxygen to the muscle and cause sharp pain.

If you have secondary dysmenorrhea, the pain is driven by other health conditions, such as:

  • Endometriosis: Uterine-like tissue growing outside the uterus.
  • Uterine Fibroids: Non-cancerous growths inside or around the uterus.
  • Adenomyosis: Uterine lining tissue growing directly into the muscular walls of the uterus.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID): A bacterial infection in the reproductive organs.

Common Signs and Symptoms

Everyone's body is different, but the most common signs of dysmenorrhea include:

  • Deep, throbbing, or cramping pain in the lower belly
  • Pain that spreads to your lower back, hips, or inner thighs
  • Nausea, vomiting, or an upset stomach (diarrhea)
  • Feeling unusually tired, weak, or dizzy
  • Headaches
  • Fainting (in extreme cases)

Are You at Risk?

While anyone who menstruates can experience painful periods, you may be more likely to have severe pain if you:

  • Are under 30 years old
  • Started your periods early (before age 11 or 12)
  • Have heavy, long, or irregular periods
  • Have a family history of painful periods
  • Smoke cigarettes
  • Have never given birth

Diagnosis: Getting the Right Health Checkup

If your cramps are severe, last for more than three days, or disrupt your normal routine, it is time to schedule a doctor consultation.

At Maria Hospital, our caring doctors will listen to your symptoms and review your medical history. To give you an accurate diagnosis and rule out secondary causes, your doctor may suggest a physical exam or safe, routine tests such as:

  • Ultrasound or MRI: Painless imaging tests to get a clear picture of your uterus and ovaries.
  • Laparoscopy: A minor procedure using a tiny camera to look directly for conditions like endometriosis.

Effective Treatment Options

You do not have to just live with the pain. Depending on the root cause and severity, your personalized treatment plan may include:

  • Medications: Anti-inflammatory painkillers (like ibuprofen) can greatly reduce cramps if taken early. Hormonal birth control (like pills or rings) can also thin the uterine lining, making periods much lighter and less painful.
  • Lifestyle Changes: Simple home remedies like using a hot water bottle, doing light aerobic exercises (like walking or yoga), and eating a balanced diet can bring natural relief.
  • Alternative Therapies: Techniques like gentle abdominal massage, acupuncture, or TENS (a small device that sends mild electrical pulses to block pain signals) help many women feel better.
  • Surgery: If your pain is caused by secondary dysmenorrhea (such as fibroids), a surgical procedure to remove the growths might be the best path to long-term relief.
