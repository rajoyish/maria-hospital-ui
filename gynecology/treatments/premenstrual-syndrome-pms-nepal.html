Premenstrual Syndrome (PMS) Treatment

For a lot of women, the days leading up to a period can be incredibly tough. While some just get a little uncomfortable, others find their whole routine turned upside down.

Here at Maria Hospital in Kathmandu, we get it. You shouldn't have to just "power through" the pain month after month. We’ve put together this guide to help you make sense of Premenstrual Syndrome (PMS) and to show you how a personalized care plan can help you get back to feeling like yourself.

So, What Exactly is PMS?

PMS is a mix of physical, emotional, and behavioral changes that tend to pop up a week or two before your period. The good news? They usually fade away a few days after your cycle starts.

It’s incredibly common—most menstruating women deal with at least one symptom. But for about 20% of us, these symptoms are severe enough to actively interfere with work, school, and relationships.

What Does PMS Look Like?

Everyone’s body is different, and your symptoms might even change from one month to the next.

How it feels physically:

  • Bloating and a gassy stomach
  • Tender, swollen breasts
  • Feeling completely wiped out
  • Cramps, backaches, or headaches
  • Sudden weight gain from water retention
  • Frustrating hormonal breakouts

How it feels emotionally:

  • Unexplained mood swings, irritability, or anger
  • Feeling extra anxious, tense, or down
  • Sudden crying spells
  • Trouble sleeping (or wanting to sleep all day)
  • Intense cravings (especially for salty or sweet snacks)
  • Brain fog or trouble concentrating

Why Does This Happen?

While the exact cause is still a bit of a medical mystery, experts believe it usually comes down to a mix of these factors:

  • Hormones: Natural shifts in your estrogen and progesterone levels.
  • Brain Chemistry: Fluctuations in serotonin, which can heavily impact your mood, sleep, and appetite.
  • Lifestyle: High stress, poor diet, and a lack of exercise can make things worse.
  • Genetics: You’re more likely to deal with PMS if it runs in your family.

A quick note on PMDD: A small group of women (about 2% to 8%) experience a much more intense version called Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD). This causes extreme mood shifts, intense anxiety, and severe depression. It is a serious medical condition, and if this sounds like you, getting professional support is crucial.

Figuring Out What's Going On

There isn't a magical blood test for PMS. Instead, when you come in for a chat at Maria Hospital, our specialists actually sit down and listen to your story.

We might ask you to track your symptoms in a simple diary for a couple of months. This just helps us confirm that your struggles are tied to your cycle and rule out other things, like a thyroid issue, that can cause similar symptoms.

Finding Your Best Treatment

You have lots of options to manage PMS, ranging from simple daily habits to advanced medical care.

1. Everyday Habits

For milder symptoms, small daily tweaks can bring huge relief:

  • Diet: Try eating smaller, frequent meals to cut down on bloating, and ease up on salt, sugar, and caffeine in the weeks before your period.
  • Movement: Just 30 minutes of walking, cycling, or swimming a few times a week works wonders for your mood and body aches.
  • Rest: Protect your 7-9 hours of sleep and try out relaxation techniques like deep breathing or yoga.

2. Vitamins & Supplements

Things like Calcium, Magnesium, and Vitamin B6 can offer real physical and emotional relief. Just make sure to chat with us at your next checkup before starting any new supplements to ensure they're the right fit for you.

3. Medical Options

If lifestyle changes aren't cutting it, we can create a treatment plan tailored specifically to your body:

  • Targeted Relief: Over-the-counter meds to knock out cramps, backaches, and headaches.
  • Hormonal Control: Birth control pills or patches to steady your hormone levels.
  • Prescription Care: Diuretics for severe bloating or antidepressants to help manage intense mood swings.
  • Therapy: Talk therapy to learn great strategies for handling stress and anxiety.
