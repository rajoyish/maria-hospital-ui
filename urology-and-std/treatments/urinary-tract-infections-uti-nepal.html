UTI (Urinary Tract Infection) Treatment

At Maria Hospital, we want you to stay informed and healthy. Urinary Tract Infections (commonly known as UTIs) are very common, but the good news is that they are easily treatable when caught early.

This article explains what a UTI is, how to spot it, and how to prevent it.

What is a UTI?

A UTI is an infection that happens in any part of your urinary system. This system includes:

Kidneys
Filters waste from your blood.
Ureters
Tubes that carry urine from the kidneys to the bladder.
Bladder
The organ that stores your urine.
Urethra
The tube that carries urine out of your body.

While an infection can happen anywhere in this system, it most often occurs in the lower part: the bladder and the urethra.

Common Symptoms

How do you know if you have a UTI? Look for these common signs:

  • Painful Urination: A burning feeling or stinging pain when you pee.
  • Frequent Urge: Feeling a strong need to pee often, even if only a small amount comes out.
  • Urine Changes: Urine that looks cloudy, red, pink, or dark (cola-colored).
  • Bad Smell: Urine that has a strong or unpleasant odor.
  • Pelvic Pain: Pain or pressure in your lower belly (especially in women).

⚠️ Important Warning: Kidney Infection

If the infection spreads to your kidneys, it becomes more serious. Please seek help immediately if you have:

  • High fever and chills.
  • Pain in your back or side.
  • Nausea and vomiting.

What Causes a UTI?

UTIs occur when germs (typically bacteria like E. coli) enter the urinary system. These bacteria usually live in the bowel but can cause problems if they travel to the urethra.

Common causes include:

  • Hygiene Habits: Wiping from "back to front" can move bacteria toward the urethra.
  • Sexual Activity: Sex can move bacteria near the urethra.
  • Holding Urine: Waiting too long to go to the bathroom allows bacteria to grow.
  • Dehydration: Not drinking enough water prevents your body from flushing out germs naturally.

Who is Most at Risk?

Anyone can get a UTI, but some people are more likely to get them than others:

  • Women: Women have a shorter urethra, making it easier for bacteria to reach the bladder.
  • Pregnant Women: Changes in the body during pregnancy increase infection risk.
  • People with Diabetes: Higher blood sugar or a weaker immune system can increase risk.
  • Menopause: Hormonal changes can lead to dryness and a higher risk of infection.
  • Kidney Stones: Stones can block the flow of urine, trapping bacteria inside.

Diagnosis and Treatment

If you have symptoms, it is important to see a doctor at Maria Hospital.

How we diagnose it

  • Urine Test: You will pee into a cup so our lab can check for bacteria.
  • Urine Culture: This tells us exactly which bacteria is causing the infection so we can choose the right medicine.

How we treat it

  • Antibiotics: These medicines kill the bacteria. Note: You must finish all your pills, even if you feel better, to stop the infection from coming back.
  • Pain Relievers: We may prescribe medicine to stop the burning sensation.
  • Fluids: We will ask you to drink plenty of water to flush your system.

Prevention: How to Stay Healthy?

You can lower your chances of getting a UTI by following these simple tips:

  • Drink Water: Aim for 6 to 8 glasses a day.
  • Wipe Correctly: Always wipe from front to back after using the toilet.
  • Don't "Hold It": Go to the bathroom when you feel the urge and empty your bladder completely.
  • Gentle Hygiene: Wear breathable cotton underwear and avoid harsh soaps or sprays in the genital area.
  • Sexual Hygiene: Peeing right after sex helps clear bacteria away.

When should you see a doctor?

Visit Maria Hospital if your symptoms last longer than a few days, if they get worse, or if you have a fever and back pain. Prompt treatment prevents complications like kidney damage.
