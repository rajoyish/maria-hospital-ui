Genital Herpes Treatment

Hearing that you might have a sexually transmitted infection (STI) can be scary, but it is important to remember that you are not alone. Genital herpes is very common, and with the right care, it is completely manageable.

At Maria Hospital, located in the heart of Kathmandu, Nepal, we believe in providing confidential and compassionate care. Here is everything you need to know about genital herpes, from recognizing the signs to getting the right treatment.

What is Genital Herpes?

Genital herpes is an infection caused by the herpes simplex virus (HSV). It typically affects the genital area, causing sores or blisters. There are two main types of the virus:

  • HSV-1: Usually causes cold sores on the lips but can spread to the genitals during oral sex.
  • HSV-2: The most common cause of genital herpes.

Once the virus enters your body, it stays there for life. However, this does not mean you will always be sick. The virus often "sleeps" in your body, and many people live healthy, normal lives with it.

How Does it Spread?

The virus spreads through skin-to-skin contact during vaginal, anal, or oral sex. It is important to know that you can get herpes even if your partner has no visible sores.

You can catch the virus through:

  • Contact with open sores or blisters.
  • Saliva, semen, or vaginal fluids.
  • Asymptomatic Shedding: When the virus is active on the skin but there are no visible signs.

You CANNOT get herpes from:

  • Toilet seats (western or squat).
  • Swimming pools.
  • Sharing towels, cups, or plates.

Common Symptoms to Watch For

Many people in Nepal have this virus without knowing it because they have no symptoms. However, if symptoms do appear, they usually happen in stages.

The First Outbreak

This is usually the most painful and can last 2 to 4 weeks. Look out for:

  • Small, fluid-filled blisters on the genitals or bottom.
  • Painful open sores that eventually scab over.
  • Pain or burning when urinating (peeing).
  • Flu-like feelings (fever, body aches, or swollen glands).

Repeat Outbreaks

After the first time, the virus may "wake up" again later. These outbreaks are usually shorter and less painful. You might feel a tingling or itching sensation (a warning sign) before sores appear.

Common Triggers: Stress, illness, tight clothing, or menstruation can sometimes trigger an outbreak.

Diagnosis and Testing

If you suspect you have herpes, it is vital to see a professional for a diagnosis. At Maria Hospital, we offer discreet testing.

  • Swab Test: If you have active sores, a doctor will gently take a sample to test for the virus. This is the most accurate method.
  • Blood Test: If no sores are present, a blood test can check for antibodies.

Do not guess your health status. A proper health checkup is the only way to be sure.

Treatment and Management

While there is currently no cure for herpes, modern medicine makes it very easy to manage. A doctor consultation will help determine the best plan for you.

  • Antiviral Medicines: Drugs like acyclovir can shorten outbreaks or prevent them from happening.
  • Daily Management: Some people take medicine every day (suppressive therapy) to lower the risk of passing the virus to a partner.

Simple Home Remedies

  • Keep the area clean with salt water.
  • Wear loose, cotton clothing to allow the skin to breathe.
  • Use an ice pack (wrapped in a cloth) to soothe pain.

Pregnancy and Family Planning

If you are pregnant or planning a baby, please tell your doctor if you or your partner have herpes.

  • Safety First: If you have an active outbreak during labor, there is a risk to the baby.
  • Medical Support: Doctors in Kathmandu, Nepal can prescribe medication late in pregnancy to prevent outbreaks, or recommend a C-section to protect your newborn.

Living a Healthy Life

A diagnosis of genital herpes does not mean your romantic life is over.

  • Use Condoms: They reduce the risk of spreading the virus (though they don't eliminate it 100%).
  • Communicate: Be honest with your partner.
  • Avoid Sex During Outbreaks: Wait until sores have completely healed.
