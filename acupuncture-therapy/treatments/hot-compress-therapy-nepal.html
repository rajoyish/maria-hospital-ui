How Does it Actually Work?

Hot compress therapy is exactly what it sounds like. We place a warm towel, a special herbal pack, a hot water bottle, or a heat lamp gently over your sore spots.

From a modern medicine point of view, heat opens up your blood vessels. This gets your blood pumping, delivering fresh oxygen and nutrients right to the damaged tissues while clearing out the buildup that causes swelling. It also helps calm spasming muscles and sensitive nerve endings so you can finally relax.

In Traditional Chinese Medicine, pain is a sign that your body's vital energy, or "Qi," and blood flow are stuck. The heat acts as warm energy to clear out cold and dampness, getting everything moving smoothly again. As the old saying goes, where there is free flow, there is no pain.

Heat or Ice?

It can be tricky to know when to use a hot pack versus an ice pack. Here is a quick guide to help you decide.

Reach for the ice if you have a fresh, sudden injury like a twisted ankle. Cold is best for the first day or two, especially if the area is red, hot, or swollen.

Grab the heat for chronic pain, stiff muscles, or old injuries that tend to ache when the weather turns cold or rainy.

Staying Safe with Heat Therapy

Heat therapy is very safe, but you still need to be careful.

  • First, only apply heat for about 15 to 30 minutes at a time.
  • Second, make sure it is comfortably warm, not burning hot. Always wrap your hot pack in a towel so you do not accidentally burn your skin.
  • You should skip the heat if you have an active skin infection, an open wound, or a new injury that is still bleeding or swollen.
  • Finally, if you have diabetes, nerve damage, or poor circulation, please talk to a doctor before trying heat therapy. You might not be able to feel if the compress is too hot.
